My Story

Computer science stuff

In addition to having a Computer Science degree, I've completed many coding projects in my CS classes. Most of them were not focused on UI, but rather on making scalabale, readable code. Making code that runs quickly is all well and good, but I've always been more interested in code that's easy to understand and slots easily into other situations.

My code for this website's navigation menu and footer are my favorite examples of this. Copying and pasting the same HTML code on each page of a given page has always bugged me--if you ever want to make a change to the menu, you'll need to go through each page and re-paste new code. Changing it so that it's generated through Javascript totally eliminated this problem! Which makes me happy.

Writing Stuff

A large portion of my free time over the years has been spent writing stories. A small portion of them are acceptable to read in a professional environment, which I've put up for view here. While this type of writing--i.e. fictional and utilizing intellectual property that isn't my own--is not what I expect to be doing for a career, this pastime has made me an exceptionally detail-cognizant editor and writer. Whether I'm focused on the correct details isn't always assured, but the time I've spent trying to get a story that existed in my head into words that somebody else could read has undoubtedly made my skills as a writer far sharper than they ever would've been.

Education Stuff

Learning and education have been important to me since quite an early age. I was part of Student Advisors For Education in middle school, an organization dedicated to giving kids with learning disabilities a platform for talking about their experiences. Ever since I've been interested in teaching, education, and learning. I took a few classes on the subject, and while it turns out I'm probably not cut out to teach children, conveying information to others is a useful skill in any field.

Work Stuff

I have had quite a few jobs outside the tech sector--two in retail and one for the United States Post Office. While the concrete things I learned to get these jobs done aren't directly relevant to technology or User Experience, the most important takeaway for me was the fractal nature of the world?

ok lemmie explain

Knowing that any package going to Rico Way is to be sorted into the sixth truck route is only important if you're working at a specific post office. But learning all those facts and forcing myself to get better at my job showed me how every aspect of the world comes from a person making an effort. Something as incidental to a daily life as the CVS where you buy Chezits is an intricate logistical balancing act of what products to order, where to display them, who to hire to put those products up, juggling all the schedules of a bunch of employees to make them happy while also keeping the store in the green...

Harboring a respect for that hard work is important, because, realistically, almost every element of the world as we know it is the product of somebody working hard to get it that way.
